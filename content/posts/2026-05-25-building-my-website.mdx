Every developer eventually builds their own website. This is mine — and I want to explain the decisions behind it.

## Why Next.js

I wanted something that would work as both a polished personal site and a sandbox for engineering experiments. Next.js 15 with the App Router gives me static generation for the public-facing content, edge functions for dynamic features, and a familiar React mental model for building interactive experiments.

The site ships as fully static HTML — fast, cacheable, and cheap to host. But under the hood there's more going on.

## Design philosophy

I spent a long time looking at sites I admire: Linear, Vercel, Stripe. What they have in common isn't a specific color palette or typeface — it's restraint. Every element earns its place.

My design rules for this site:

- **No color accent.** Neutral palette only. Sophistication through spacing and type, not color.
- **Light mode.** Clean and approachable — important when the audience includes product people, not just engineers.
- **Geist font.** Sharp, technical, not cold. Works equally well for headings and code.
- **Subtle motion only.** Entrance animations under 400ms. Hover effects under 150ms. No parallax.

## The hidden engineering

The visible site is simple. The engineering underneath is intentionally overkill.

Blog post view counts are tracked in Vercel KV (Redis), incremented via an Edge Route Handler with a sliding window rate limiter. This is a textbook distributed systems pattern running on real infrastructure — and it cost me nothing to set up.

There are also easter eggs hidden in the site. I won't spoil them here.

## What's next

This site is meant to grow. Future experiments include WebSockets for real-time features, a WASM module, and maybe a CRDT-based collaborative feature hidden behind a secret route.

For now: the foundation is solid, the design is clean, and the blog is live.
